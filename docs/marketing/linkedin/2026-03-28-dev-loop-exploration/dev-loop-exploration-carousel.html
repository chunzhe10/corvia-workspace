Layer 1 in Action
"Knowledge Should
Have a Lifecycle"
From a vague direction to a complete design.
Three Approaches Explored
A: Score-Based Decay
Fixed thresholds. Simple. But no way to promote knowledge that becomes relevant again.
Rejected: too rigid
B: Usage-Driven Tiering
Promotion paths based on access. Flexible. But no decay for unused entries.
Rejected: no forgetting
C: Hybrid + Policies
Composite scoring with configurable forgetting. Both decay and promotion.
Selected: best of both
The RFC Defined
• 5 memory types (Structural, Decisional, Episodic, Analytical, Procedural)
• 4-tier lifecycle (Hot → Warm → Cold → Forgotten)
• Composite retention scoring with hysteresis to prevent oscillation
• Configurable forgetting policies per scope and type

Research That Compounds
Nothing Gets
Lost Between Sessions
Every brainstorm, every decision, every rejected alternative gets persisted. When I revisit a topic, I see what I explored before and what changed since.
❌
Without Persistence
• Re-explore the same topic from scratch
• Forget why an approach was rejected
• Miss when the landscape changed
• Knowledge evaporates between sessions
✅
With Persistent Memory
• See what was explored before
• Understand why decisions were made
• Track what changed since last visit
• Knowledge compounds over time
How It Works
I use corvia (the project I'm building) to persist all research. Claude queries the knowledge base at the start of every session. Prior decisions, rejected alternatives, and design rationale are all searchable.

When I revisited the retrieval pipeline three weeks after the initial design, corvia surfaced what I'd already explored and what had changed. The exploration picked up where it left off instead of starting from scratch.

Key Takeaways
What Actually
Matters
💡
The thinking is the hard part
Layer 1 is where I learn. Exploring trade-offs, challenging assumptions, making judgment calls. The implementation is the easy part.
📈
Design review prevents rewrites
Reviewing the RFC before coding is why 13 PRs merged with 0 rewrites. Catching a flaw in a spec costs minutes. In code, it costs hours.
🐘
Research compounds when persisted
Saving every brainstorm and rejected alternative means revisiting a topic is faster each time. Knowledge grows across sessions, not within them.
⚡
Well-defined issues execute themselves
When Layer 1 is thorough, Layer 2 is almost boring. The dev-loop validates, implements, reviews, and merges. That's the point.
